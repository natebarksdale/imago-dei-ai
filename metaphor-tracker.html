Why Track Metaphors

Metaphors aren't just decorative language – they fundamentally shape how we think about AI, human nature, and technology's role in society. The metaphors speakers use reveal deep assumptions about consciousness, agency, creativity, and what makes us human.

Pay special attention to how metaphors influence epistemology (what we accept as true, how we handle AI "hallucinations") and cultural horizons (what seems possible, necessary, or inevitable). As McLuhan observed, every technological extension involves an amputation—what human capacities might we be losing as AI capabilities expand? How does AI gradually shift the Overton window of acceptable practice?

This tracker helps you identify and analyze the metaphorical frameworks that underlie conference discussions.

Technology & Machine Metaphors
How we describe AI and computational processes
▼
"AI Brain" / "Digital Brain"
"The AI has learned..." "Neural networks process information"
Treats computational processes as analogous to human cognition, suggesting consciousness and intentionality in AI systems.
What it reveals:
Assumes cognition is fundamentally computational. May obscure differences between pattern matching and understanding.
"Machine Learning"
"The model learns from data" "Training algorithms"
Applies pedagogical metaphors to statistical optimization, suggesting AI systems acquire knowledge like students.
What it conceals:
Machines don't learn through understanding but through pattern recognition. May conflate information processing with wisdom.
"System Architecture"
"Building AI systems" "Infrastructure" "Foundations"
Describes AI development using construction metaphors, emphasizing stability, design, and engineering.
What it emphasizes:
Technical mastery and control. May obscure emergent behaviors and unintended consequences.
"AI Evolution" / "Genetic Algorithms"
"AI is evolving" "Survival of the fittest algorithms"
Uses biological evolution metaphors for algorithmic optimization, suggesting natural selection and adaptation.
Theological implications:
May challenge or support evolutionary vs. design arguments about intelligence and purpose.
"AI Companions" / "AI Assistants"
"Your AI friend" "Digital assistants help us"
Applies relational metaphors to human-AI interaction, suggesting genuine social relationships.
What it assumes:
AI can participate in meaningful relationships. May commodify or instrumentalize human relationship patterns.
"Training Data" / "Feeding the Machine"
"AI consumes data" "Hungry for information"
Combines nutritional and educational metaphors for data processing, suggesting AI systems have appetites and needs.
Cultural patterns:
Reflects how AI systems "digest" human cultural output, potentially reproducing cultural biases and patterns.

Historical Precedents

Printing Press: "Impression," "type," "character" – metaphors from printing shaped how we think about personality and identity.

Photography: "Developing," "exposure," "negative" – shaped concepts of memory, truth, and representation that we now apply to digital media.

Telegraph: "Networks," "connections," "transmission" – created metaphors for communication we still use for internet and AI systems.

Human Nature & Anthropological Metaphors
How we describe human capabilities vs. AI
▼
"Biological Computer"
"The human brain as wetware" "Biological processing"
Describes human cognition using computational metaphors, suggesting equivalence between human and artificial intelligence.
Theological tension:
Challenges distinctiveness of human consciousness and soul. May reduce humans to sophisticated machines.
"Image of God" / "Divine Spark"
"Humans bear God's image" "Divine creativity"
Theological metaphors emphasizing human uniqueness, dignity, and special relationship with the divine.
What it protects:
Human dignity and uniqueness. Suggests aspects of humanity that cannot be replicated or replaced by AI.
"Human Resources"
"Optimizing human capital" "Resource allocation"
Economic metaphors that treat humans as inputs to be optimized, similar to computational resources.
Dehumanizing risk:
May instrumentalize human workers and prepare for human-AI substitution in economic systems.
"Emotional Intelligence"
"EQ vs IQ" "Processing emotions"
Applies intelligence metaphors to emotional life, suggesting emotions can be systematized and quantified.
What it assumes:
Emotions are cognitive processes that can be analyzed and potentially replicated by AI systems.
"Soul vs. Algorithm"
"Soulless machines" "The human soul"
Contrasts transcendent human essence with mechanical processes, defending human distinctiveness.
Boundary marking:
Identifies aspects of humanity (consciousness, spirituality, moral agency) that resist technological replication.
"Human-in-the-Loop"
"Human oversight" "Keeping humans in control"
Positions humans as supervisors or quality controllers in AI systems, maintaining human authority.
Power dynamics:
Suggests humans retain ultimate control and responsibility, but may also imply humans are just another system component.
Epistemology & Truth Metaphors
How we judge what is true and how AI challenges knowledge
▼
"AI Hallucination"
"The model hallucinated" "Confabulating facts"
Applies psychological/medical metaphors to AI errors, treating false outputs as perceptual distortions rather than probabilistic misfires.
What it obscures:
AI doesn't perceive or hallucinate—it generates plausible-sounding patterns. This metaphor anthropomorphizes statistical processes and may obscure the fundamental difference between human error and algorithmic pattern-matching.
"Confabulation" / "Making Things Up"
"The AI made that up" "Fabricating information"
Uses deception metaphors for AI errors, suggesting intentional dishonesty or creativity rather than probabilistic generation.
Epistemological risk:
Treats AI outputs as truth claims requiring verification, shifting burden of epistemic responsibility from developers to users. Changes how we think about authority and trustworthiness.
"Ground Truth" / "Training on Reality"
"Comparing to ground truth" "Learning from real data"
Spatial metaphors that position certain data as foundational reality, obscuring whose reality and which truths are represented.
Power dynamics:
Who determines what counts as "ground truth"? Whose experiences and knowledge are treated as authoritative foundations for AI training?
"Synthetic Truth" / "Artificial Facts"
"AI-generated knowledge" "Synthetic information"
Manufacturing metaphors applied to information creation, suggesting truth can be fabricated or assembled from components.
Theological concerns:
Challenges correspondence theory of truth. If AI "creates" plausible facts, does truth become merely coherence or consensus rather than correspondence to reality?
"Source of Truth" / "Single Source"
"AI as authoritative source" "Definitive answers"
Religious/philosophical metaphors positioning AI systems as authoritative origins of reliable information.
Authority shift:
Displaces traditional epistemic authorities (experts, institutions, scriptures) with algorithmic ones. What does this mean for Christian claims about revelation and truth?
"Pattern Recognition" / "Seeing Patterns"
"AI detects patterns" "Finding hidden connections"
Visual metaphors for statistical correlation, suggesting AI "sees" what humans miss.
Epistemological assumption:
Correlation becomes causation; patterns become meaning. May privilege statistical patterns over human wisdom, experience, and moral reasoning.
Cultural Transformation & Horizon Shifting
How AI reshapes what seems possible, necessary, or thinkable
▼
"Overton Window" / "Shifting Norms"
"AI normalizes the unthinkable" "Moving the window"
Political metaphor applied to how AI gradually shifts boundaries of acceptable thought and practice, making previously unthinkable options seem normal.
Moral formation:
AI doesn't just respond to culture—it actively reshapes moral intuitions by normalizing certain choices. What was unthinkable (AI companions, algorithmic justice) becomes inevitable.
"McLuhan's Amputation"
"Every extension is an amputation" "What we lose when we gain"
Media theory metaphor: every tool that extends human capability simultaneously amputates or atrophies what it replaces.
What AI amputates:
AI writing tools may atrophy our capacity for sustained thought. AI memory may diminish human remembering. AI relationship simulators may weaken capacity for human intimacy. What are we losing as we gain?
"Technological Lock-In"
"Path dependency" "No going back"
Economic metaphors for how technological choices become irreversible, constraining future possibilities.
Freedom and agency:
Once AI is integrated into infrastructure (healthcare, education, justice), can we choose otherwise? Does technological "progress" narrow rather than expand human freedom?
"Horizon of Possibility"
"Expanding possibilities" "New frontiers"
Spatial metaphors suggesting AI opens new territories of human potential and achievement.
What it conceals:
While appearing to expand horizons, AI may actually constrain them—replacing human capabilities with optimized algorithms, reducing diverse ways of living to data-driven norms.
"Echo Chambers" / "Filter Bubbles"
"Algorithmic echo chambers" "Information silos"
Uses spatial metaphors for how AI systems shape information consumption and social interaction.
Community implications:
Suggests AI systems fragment rather than unite communities, challenging Christian ideals of unity and understanding.
"Data Mining" / "Information Harvesting"
"Mining user data" "Harvesting information"
Agricultural and extractive metaphors for collecting human behavioral data and cultural patterns.
Power dynamics:
Treats human cultural expression as raw material to be extracted and processed for commercial purposes.
"Algorithmic Bias" / "AI Poisoning"
"Biased algorithms" "Poisoned training data"
Medical and moral metaphors for how AI systems inherit and amplify human prejudices and cultural patterns.
Original sin parallel:
Suggests systemic corruption that spreads through AI systems, similar to theological concepts of inherited sin.
"Normalization" / "The New Normal"
"AI is becoming normal" "Normalized surveillance"
Statistical metaphors that describe gradual cultural acceptance of previously controversial practices.
Moral drift:
What seemed dystopian becomes mundane. AI surveillance, automated decision-making, synthetic relationships shift from science fiction to accepted reality without deliberate choice.
"Tech Evangelism" / "AI Gospel"
"Spreading the AI gospel" "Technology evangelists"
Religious metaphors applied to technology promotion, suggesting tech adoption as conversion or salvation.
Quasi-religious dynamics:
Technology becomes object of faith and devotion, potentially competing with or replacing traditional religious commitment.
"Tool vs. Prosthetic"
"AI as tool" "AI as extension of ourselves"
Distinguishes between external instruments and integrated extensions. A tool you use; a prosthetic becomes part of you.
Identity and integration:
When does AI shift from tool to prosthetic? What happens to human identity when AI memory, judgment, or creativity becomes integrated into our cognitive processes?

Divine providence metaphors applied to how AI systems shape human choices and life outcomes.
Agency questions:
Do AI systems guide human life in ways analogous to divine providence, or do they usurp human agency and freedom

Printing Press Legacy
"Impression," "Type," "Character," "Edition"
Printing metaphors shaped concepts of personality, reproduction, and authority that we now apply to AI-generated content.
Authority questions:
What makes an "original" vs. "copy" when AI generates contentHow do we understand authorship and authenticity
Photography Metaphors
"Developing," "Exposure," "Negative," "Focus"
Photography gave us metaphors for memory, truth, and representation that shape how we think about AI and reality.
Truth and reality:
How do photography metaphors help or hinder our understanding of AI-generated images and "deepfakes"
Industrial Revolution
"Efficiency," "Optimization," "Automation," "Productivity"
Industrial metaphors emphasize efficiency and optimization, potentially reducing human work to mechanical processes.
Human dignity:
Do industrial metaphors prepare us to replace human workers with AI, or can they help us imagine human-AI collaboration
Telegraph & Telephone
"Networks," "Connections," "Transmission," "Signal"
Communication technology metaphors that shape how we understand AI systems and internet connectivity.
Relationship implications:
How do communication metaphors affect our understanding of human-AI interaction and AI-mediated relationships
Clockwork Metaphors
"Mechanism," "Clockwork precision," "Gears," "Programming"
Mechanical metaphors from clockmaking that emphasize precision, predictability, and deterministic operation.
Predictability vs. emergence:
Do clockwork metaphors help us understand AI systems, or do they obscure emergent and unpredictable behaviors
Weaving & Textiles
"Web," "Thread," "Pattern," "Fabric"
Textile metaphors that emphasize interconnection, pattern, and the weaving together of multiple elements.
Community and culture:
How do weaving metaphors help us understand how AI systems are woven into the fabric of society and culture
